\section{Evaluation}

\subsection{Evaluation Setup}
We evaluate all methods under a strictly matched protocol.
Passenger demand is constructed from NYC yellow taxi trip data (November 2025).
The simulation window is fixed to 06:00--22:00 with 15-minute slots and receding horizon $H=64$.
Because real battery swapping station operation logs are not available in this study, station-side operations are simulated with parameterized station resources (battery inventory, charging slots, and swap service capacity).
All methods use the same demand input, seed policy, and three predefined resource settings.
Evaluation dates are fixed to 2025-11-01, 2025-11-05, and 2025-11-09.

We compare the proposed \textbf{COP} (Coordinated Optimization Policy) with three baselines:
\textbf{FDB} (Fleet Dispatch Baseline),
\textbf{HDB} (Heuristic Dispatch Baseline), and
\textbf{ODB} (Oracle Dispatch Baseline).
FDB performs fleet-level dispatch and sends low-energy taxis to swapping stations, while stations use first-come-first-served charging.
HDB lets each taxi independently choose passenger-search actions and low-energy swapping decisions, while stations also use first-come-first-served charging.
ODB uses oracle-style dispatch with future demand information while keeping the same first-come-first-served charging rule as HDB.
We additionally define \textbf{CCB} (Capacity-Constrained Charging Baseline), which keeps fleet-level dispatch but constrains the maximum number of simultaneously active chargers (e.g., 90\% of nominal chargers).

The primary endpoint is total\_served; secondary metrics are used to characterize operational trade-offs.

\subsection{Primary Results}
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:primary_served} and Table~\ref{tab:primary_aggregate},
COP improves total\_served by $+113.9\%$ over HDB and $+14.4\%$ over ODB.
Compared with FDB, COP improves total\_served by $+0.22\%$.

The paired evidence is consistent:
COP is higher than HDB in all 9 matched units and higher than ODB in all 9 matched units.
Two-sided sign tests give $p=0.0039$ in both comparisons.
Against FDB, COP is higher in 6 matched units and lower in 3, with no significant paired difference ($p=0.5078$).

The service gain is accompanied by trade-offs:
COP shows lower swap success and higher swap deadline miss than the baselines,
while achieving the lowest charging miss ratio.
This is consistent with a service-priority operating regime under Eq.~(10), rather than uniform improvement on every metric.

\subsection{Ablation Evidence}
From the ablation study, dispatch policy is the dominant source of total\_served variation, while charging-policy effects are secondary and mixed.
The direct COP vs FDB ablation contrast is $+171.1$ served trips on average, consistent with the small aggregate gain above.

\subsection{Capacity-Constrained Scenario}
We evaluate CCB (Capacity-Constrained Charging Baseline) in a supplemental scenario.
CCB keeps fleet-level dispatch with FCFS charging but limits active chargers to an integerized cap90 setting (3$\rightarrow$2 and 5$\rightarrow$4 chargers per station across resource settings).
This scenario changes available infrastructure capacity and is therefore reported as a supplemental robustness check rather than part of the primary paired-significance conclusion.

Under CCB, mean total\_served drops by $-53.55\%$ relative to FDB and $-53.65\%$ relative to COP.
Charging miss increases substantially, while peak station power is reduced by 30~kW.
The lower waiting count under CCB should be interpreted jointly with the large service drop.

\subsection{Protocol-Aligned Time-Series Check}
Figure~\ref{fig:ts_panel} shows a protocol-aligned replay on 2025-11-05
under one representative resource setting, comparing COP and HDB.
The cumulative served difference is $+44947$ (COP minus HDB), consistent with aggregate results.
This replay is used for mechanism illustration, not as a replacement for paired statistical inference.
